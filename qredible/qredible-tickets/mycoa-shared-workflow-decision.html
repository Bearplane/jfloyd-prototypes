MyCOA Setup Wizard & Shared Workflow — what we can ship now vs. what needs more planning

Why you’re reading this: During the April 9 meetings, the team discussed launching MyCOA as a self-service product for merchants — starting with Westtown’s 545 merchants. The conversation revealed that what sounds like “build a setup wizard” actually touches much deeper questions about how merchants and processors share data on the platform. This document lays out the situation clearly so we can make a decision together on how to move forward.

Revenue opportunity
Westtown: 545 merchants, mandatory MyCOA — ~$32K/month potential
What we can ship fast
Setup wizard, branded template, paywall — merchant manages their own COAs
What needs more work
Real-time collaboration between merchant and processor — shared data, shared controls

The key question for the team

Are we building a setup wizard so merchants can manage their own MyCOA? (Simpler, faster to ship)
Or are we also building a shared workspace where merchants and processors collaborate on the same COAs in real time? (Much larger effort)

Already in progress (separate from this decision)

Black Bear branded template — hardcoded template for coa.blackbeardispensary.com. On track for delivery this week. Does not depend on the wizard or shared workflow.

2. What the platform can and cannot do today

An honest look at what exists vs. what would need to be built — in plain terms.

Current reality

What works today

  • MyCOA page & publishing: A company can upload COAs, assign them to products, and publish them on a branded public page.
  • Processor management of merchant data: Processors like Westtown and Flex can add merchants to their We Monitor list, scan their websites, and ingest products and COAs directly into that merchant’s company record. The processor has full create, edit, and delete permissions on those products and COAs with no restrictions. Every COA includes an audit trail showing who uploaded or modified each field.
  • Subscription plans & paywall: We have a plan catalog with pricing tiers and a paywall screen that shows plan options.
  • Company invites: We can invite users to join a company and link companies to processors.
  • Product scanning & COA extraction: The system can scan merchant websites for products and extract COA data automatically.

What does not exist yet

  • No permission controls on shared data: The processor and merchant already work against the same products and COAs (stored under the merchant’s company). But once a merchant is invited as a user, both parties have full unrestricted access — there is no way to say “the merchant can upload but the processor can delete” or vice versa. There are zero permission controls governing the relationship.
  • No granular permissions: The system has basic roles (Admin, User) but no fine-grained controls like “can upload but not delete” or “can view but not edit.”
  • No product limit concept: Product count limits (e.g., “25 products max”) do not exist anywhere in the system — not as a field on subscription plans, not as a configuration, and not as any form of enforcement. This would need to be designed as a new feature of the subscription plan system and then enforced on the server.
  • No invite-to-wizard pipeline: Today’s invite links just add someone to the platform. They cannot trigger a setup wizard, pre-select a plan, or tailor the paywall.
  • No cross-company notifications: When one company publishes a COA, other companies watching that merchant are not automatically notified.
How it works under the hood (simplified): Every product and COA in the system belongs to exactly one company — the merchant’s company. When a processor like Westtown monitors a merchant, the processor works directly on the merchant’s data: scanning websites, creating products, ingesting COAs, editing fields, and deleting records. The processor operates on the merchant’s company record with full permissions. An audit trail on each COA tracks who uploaded or modified it. The products and COAs visible in We Monitor’s Product Compliance tab are the same records visible in that merchant’s MyCOA view.
Technical: Products/COAs scoped by pl_company_id (the merchant’s company). MP accesses client data via CompanyParamPolicyHandler + pl_merchant_processor_client relationship with full CRUD. MyCOAProvider uses plCompanyId only — no MP context awareness.
Why plan limits matter: If we want to offer subscription tiers (25 products for $X, 100 for $Y), we need a way to define and enforce those limits. Today, no product-count limit concept exists anywhere in the system. The subscription plan model supports feature visibility flags (showing/hiding UI elements per plan), but it has no mechanism for numeric caps like “25 products max.” Both the data model (adding a limit field to plans) and the enforcement (checking it server-side on every product-creation path — direct upload, product scan, COA extraction) would need to be built from scratch.
Technical: No product-count limit field exists on pl_plan, pl_plan_feature, or PlanFeatureConfigDto. clientThreshold on plan features is used for monitoring slot counts (watchlist), not product counts. No limit check in upsertProduct (API), ProductScan.service (Middleware), or CoaExtractionExport.service (Middleware).

3. Where the requested workflow breaks down

Walking through each part of the vision and showing what works vs. what doesn’t — with real scenarios.

Gap analysis

What we want to happen

Invite: Westtown sends a co-branded invite link to a merchant. That link starts the MyCOA wizard and connects the merchant to the right subscription plan.

Wizard: The merchant goes through a branded step-by-step setup (company name, logo, colors) and then sees a tailored paywall showing only the plans relevant to them.

Controlled access: After onboarding, the merchant can see the products and COAs that Westtown already collected (which already live under the merchant’s company). Both parties work on the same data, but with appropriate permission controls — e.g., the merchant can upload and publish COAs, while the processor retains oversight.

Auto-notify: When the merchant publishes a COA, Westtown is notified automatically and their compliance dashboard updates in real time.

What actually happens today

Invite: Today’s invite links add a user to the platform, but they cannot pre-select a plan, trigger a wizard, or carry any MyCOA-specific context. The merchant lands on a generic onboarding screen.

Wizard: No setup wizard exists yet. The wizard has been designed (see the Netlify prototype) but not built into the platform.

Controlled access: The data already exists under the merchant’s company — put there by the processor’s scans. Once the merchant is invited as a user, they will see all of it. But both parties get full unrestricted access with no permission differentiation. There is no way to control what the merchant can vs. cannot do relative to the processor, or vice versa. The audit trail tracks who did what, but there are no guardrails.

Auto-notify: No cross-company notification system exists. Real-time updates (like scan progress) are sent within a single company, not between companies.

The data-sharing dilemma (QP-3226)

There is a deeper conflict here. We have a separate ticket (QP-3226) that requires us to isolate data between processors — meaning if both Flex and Westtown monitor the same merchant, they should not see each other’s scan results, notes, or approval decisions.

But the MyCOA shared workflow asks us to do the opposite: let a merchant see data that a processor collected on their behalf.

These two goals conflict. If we fully isolate data between companies (as QP-3226 requires), then sharing data between a merchant and their processor becomes harder. If we build the shared workflow first without isolation, we risk exposing one processor’s work to another.

Example: Suppose Flex and Westtown both monitor “Green Valley CBD.” Westtown scans the website and finds 200 products. Flex does the same scan independently and finds 200 products with their own notes and compliance decisions. If Green Valley joins MyCOA through Westtown’s invite, which set of products do they see? Do they see Flex’s data too? What if they edit a product — does it change for both processors?

Bottom line: We need to decide the data-sharing model before we can safely build the shared merchant/processor workflow. Rushing this decision risks creating data leaks between processors or locking ourselves into an architecture that is expensive to change later.

4. Open questions the team must answer

These are blocking decisions. Each one affects how we build this and what gets delivered.

Decisions needed

Q1: Does the merchant get immediate access to processor-populated data?

The products and COAs that Westtown collected by scanning already live under the merchant’s company. When the merchant is invited as a user, do they immediately see and work with that data?

If yes (use existing data): No data transfer is needed — the records are already there. But we need to decide on permission controls (see Q2), because right now both parties would have full unrestricted access. We also need to consider what happens when multiple processors monitor the same merchant (see the QP-3226 dilemma below).
If no (clean slate): We would need to hide or separate the processor’s existing data from the merchant’s MyCOA view, even though it already lives under their company. The merchant starts fresh and uploads their own COAs. This avoids the permission question for now but means duplicated effort and potentially conflicting product/COA records.

Q2: Do we need permission controls once the merchant joins?

Today, the processor has full unrestricted access to the merchant’s products and COAs (create, edit, delete, reassign). Once the merchant is invited as a user of their own company, they will also have full unrestricted access to the same data. Do we need to differentiate what each party can do?

If we add permissions: We need a permission system that does not exist today. Every action (edit, delete, publish, reassign) would need to check who is doing it and what their relationship is to the data. This is a significant new capability to build.
If we keep it unrestricted: Both the processor and the merchant can do anything to any product or COA, just as the processor can today. Simpler, but risks conflicts (e.g., the processor deletes a product the merchant just uploaded a COA for). The audit trail shows who did what, but there are no guardrails.

Q3: Do we show subscription tiers the merchant can’t use?

If a merchant has 200 products, do we show them a 25-product tier on the paywall? What happens if they pick it?

If we show all tiers: We need to design product count limits as a new concept in the subscription plan system (no such field exists today), then enforce those limits on the server every time a product is created — through upload, scan, or COA extraction. This is net-new functionality.
If we hide irrelevant tiers: We need to know the merchant’s product count before showing the paywall, which means we either pre-scan their website or ask them. Adds a step to the onboarding flow.

Q4: Is this invite separate from the future compliance portal invite?

We are also planning a compliance portal for merchants. Will the MyCOA invite and the compliance portal invite be the same link, or separate?

If same invite: We need a configurable invite system that can trigger different onboarding flows (MyCOA wizard vs. compliance portal setup) based on context. More flexible but more complex to build.
If separate invites: Simpler for now. The MyCOA invite only triggers the MyCOA wizard. The compliance portal invite is built later as its own flow. Risk: merchants may get two separate invite emails from the same processor.

Q5: Can a MyCOA merchant run product scans on their own website?

Product scans automatically discover products from a website. Should MyCOA merchants have access to this feature?

If yes: A scan could bring in hundreds of products instantly, potentially exceeding whatever product limit we define for the merchant’s plan. We would need to build the product-limit concept first (see Q3) and enforce it server-side before enabling this.
If no: Merchants manually manage their product list and upload COAs. Simpler, and avoids the plan-limit enforcement problem for now.

Q6: When a merchant publishes a COA, does it replace the processor’s product scan?

The April 9 meeting discussed replacing the “Scan for COAs” button with a “MyCOA Enabled” badge once a merchant is onboarded. Does the system stop scanning that merchant?

If yes: We need a per-merchant flag that tells the processor’s monitoring system to stop scanning and start trusting the merchant’s MyCOA uploads instead. New logic needed in the scan workflow.
If no: The processor keeps scanning as usual, and MyCOA is just an additional data source. Simpler, but creates a confusing situation where the processor sees scan results AND MyCOA-published COAs for the same merchant.

Recommendation: Option C — Phase it

Ship the MyCOA setup wizard, paywall, and branded template now as Phase 1. This gets merchants onboarded and paying, and gives Westtown a real product to roll out to their 545 merchants.

Then design the shared merchant/processor workflow as Phase 2, after the open questions in Section 4 are answered and the QP-3226 data isolation approach is settled. This avoids building something we might have to tear apart later.

Why this is the right call

  • The wizard itself is ready to build — designs exist, the tech is straightforward.
  • The shared workflow has unanswered questions that affect the entire platform architecture.
  • Rushing the shared model risks conflicting with data isolation work (QP-3226) already on the roadmap.
  • Phase 1 is valuable on its own — merchants get branded COA pages and self-service management.
  • Splitting the work is honest about what’s ready and what isn’t.

7. What must be resolved before Phase 2 can start

These are the building blocks. Each one is a decision or design that Phase 2 depends on.

Phase 2 prerequisites

1. Data ownership decision

Does the merchant own all data outright, or do we create a shared data layer where both processor and merchant can contribute? This is the single most important architectural decision and affects every other piece.

2. Permission system design

Today we have Admin and User roles — nothing more. Phase 2 needs relationship-aware permissions: “This processor can view but not edit this merchant’s COAs.” This requires a new permission framework.

3. QP-3226 alignment

The data isolation ticket (QP-3226) and the shared workflow pull in opposite directions. We need to design both together so they don’t conflict. The isolation work should not accidentally block the collaboration features we want later.

4. Invite system redesign

Invites need to carry context: what type of onboarding flow, which plan options, which processor is inviting. Today’s invite links are generic. This needs a new invite architecture.

5. Product/COA limit system

Product count limits do not exist as a concept anywhere in the system today — not as a data field, not as a configuration, not as enforcement. A new limiting-factor feature must be designed, added to the subscription plan data model, and then enforced server-side across all product-creation paths (direct upload, product scan, COA extraction, bulk import).

6. Cross-company event system

Real-time notifications between companies (e.g., “Merchant X just published a new COA”) require a new event routing system. Today, real-time events only flow within a single company.

Use these points to frame your thinking and feedback.

Discussion guide

What we can ship right now

A real setup wizard, a paywall with subscription plans, and a branded MyCOA page for every merchant. The merchant manages their own COAs independently. This is Phase 1 and can be delivered in 2–3 weeks.

What needs more thought first

The shared workflow where merchants and processors collaborate on the same data in real time. This is a bigger architectural change that has unanswered questions and conflicts with other work on the roadmap (QP-3226).

The recommended path

Ship Phase 1 now to start generating revenue and get merchants onboarded. Then take the time to properly design Phase 2 with the full team, making sure the sharing model and isolation model work together.

What we need from the team

Agreement on the phased approach, and then answers to the 6 open questions in Section 4. Those answers will shape the Phase 2 design and determine how long it takes.
